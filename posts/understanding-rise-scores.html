Your child's RISE scores explained: what the numbers mean, what they don't, and what to do next.

A parent-friendly guide to understanding Utah's RISE test results — from a teacher who's sat across from hundreds of families at conference tables.

UC
The Utah Core Room
Utah educator & resource creator

Sometime in the next few weeks, your child will come home with a piece of paper — or you'll get an email with a link — containing their RISE test results. And if you're like most parents I've talked to over the years, your first reaction will be some combination of confusion, relief, or quiet worry. Maybe all three at once.

The scores aren't exactly intuitive. There are numbers, percentiles, performance levels, and phrases like "approaching proficiency" that could mean almost anything. I've watched parents stare at these reports at conference tables trying to figure out whether their child is doing well or falling behind — and honestly, I don't blame them. The reports weren't designed for parents. They were designed for data systems.

So let me translate. Here's what the numbers actually mean, what they don't mean, and what you can realistically do with the information.

What the RISE test is actually measuring

RISE stands for Readiness Improvement Success Empowerment, and it's Utah's state assessment for students in grades 3 through 8. Your child takes it in ELA (English Language Arts), math, and depending on grade level, science. The test is aligned to Utah Core Standards — meaning it's designed to measure the specific skills that Utah says students should learn at each grade level.

In ELA, for example, the test measures reading comprehension across fiction and nonfiction texts, vocabulary knowledge, the ability to find and cite text evidence, and understanding of text structure and author's craft. In math, it covers grade-level operations, problem-solving, and mathematical reasoning. In science, it aligns to the Utah SEEd standards.

What it does NOT measure: your child's intelligence, their creativity, their work ethic, their character, their potential, or their value as a human being. I say this not as a platitude but as a professional reminder from someone who has watched too many families attach their child's identity to a number on a screen.

Understanding the score report: what the numbers mean

Your child's report will show a few key pieces of information. Here's how to read each one.

The overall score is a number on a scale. By itself, this number doesn't tell you much — it's not like a percentage where 90 means an A. Instead, the score is compared against cut points that determine which performance level your child falls into.

Performance levels are the most useful part of the report. Utah uses four levels. Level 1 (Below Proficient) means your child is not yet demonstrating grade-level mastery of the tested skills. Level 2 (Approaching Proficient) means they're getting closer but haven't consistently met the standard. Level 3 (Proficient) means they're meeting grade-level expectations — this is the target. Level 4 (Highly Proficient) means they're exceeding expectations and demonstrating advanced skills.

Here's the nuance most reports don't explain: a Level 2 does not mean your child is failing. It means there are specific skill areas where they need more practice or support. A student at Level 2 might be strong in vocabulary but struggling with inference — the overall number won't tell you that, but the sub-scores will.

Sub-scores or strand scores break the overall score into categories. In ELA, you might see separate scores for Reading Literature, Reading Informational Text, and Language. These are gold. A child who scores well in Literature but low in Informational Text doesn't need "more reading help" — they need specific practice with nonfiction passages. That precision matters enormously for figuring out what to do next.

What the scores DON'T tell you

They don't tell you how your child performed compared to the student sitting next to them. RISE is criterion-referenced, meaning your child is measured against the standards, not against other students. A classroom where every student scores Level 3 is possible — it's not graded on a curve.

They don't account for test-day factors. If your child was anxious, tired, hungry, distracted by a bee in the testing room (it happens more than you'd think), or simply having an off day, the score reflects that single moment, not their ability.

They don't capture growth. A student who started the year reading two grade levels below and ended at one grade level below made extraordinary progress. The score report might still show Level 2 — but the trajectory is something to celebrate, not worry about.

What to do with the information: 5 practical steps

Step 1: Look at the sub-scores before the overall score. The overall number tells you almost nothing actionable. The sub-scores tell you exactly which skills need attention. If your child scored well in everything except vocabulary, that's a focused, solvable problem — not a crisis.

Step 2: Compare this year to last year. If your child moved from Level 1 to Level 2, that's significant growth even though they haven't hit Level 3 yet. If they stayed at Level 3 but the sub-scores shifted, look at what improved and what dipped. Growth matters more than any single snapshot.

Step 3: Have a conversation with your child's teacher. Bring the report and ask: "What do these sub-scores tell you about where my child needs support?" A good teacher will already know the answer and can suggest specific strategies — both for school and for home. You don't need to be an expert; you just need to ask the right question.

Step 4: Focus on one or two areas, not everything. If the report shows three areas below proficient, don't try to fix all three simultaneously. Pick the one that's closest to proficiency (the easiest win) and concentrate there. Small, focused practice builds momentum better than scattered, overwhelming review.

Step 5: Keep reading together. This applies regardless of the scores. Reading with your child — or having them read independently while you read your own book nearby — remains the single most impactful thing a parent can do for literacy development. It builds vocabulary, comprehension, fluency, and the kind of stamina that standardized tests require. Twenty minutes a day, any book they choose, no quizzing afterward. Just reading.

When to be concerned (and when not to)

A single year at Level 2 in one subject is not cause for alarm, especially if your child is showing growth. Many students develop at different rates, and a score that's slightly below the proficiency cut point may reflect a student who's nearly there and just needs a bit more time and targeted practice.

If your child has scored Level 1 for multiple years in a row across subjects, that's a pattern worth discussing with their school. Ask about intervention programs, tutoring options, and whether an evaluation for learning differences might be appropriate. Schools have resources for this — but they sometimes wait for parents to ask.

If your child scored Level 3 or 4, celebrate that and keep doing what you're doing. You don't need to "push harder" or add extra tutoring. Proficient means proficient. Let your child enjoy the accomplishment and continue building their love of learning without the pressure to perform beyond grade level unless they want to.

The bigger picture

I've sat across from hundreds of parents at conference tables with test reports between us. The question I hear most often is: "Is my child going to be okay?" The answer, almost always, is yes. These scores are one data point in a very long educational journey. They help us identify where to focus next — nothing more, nothing less.

Your child is not a score. They're a person who is growing, learning, struggling with some things, excelling at others, and figuring out who they are. The test measured whether they could identify the main idea of a passage on a Tuesday in April. It did not measure whether they're kind, curious, creative, or brave.

Use the data. Don't worship it. And if the numbers aren't where you hoped, remember: the most powerful intervention isn't a workbook or a tutor. It's a parent who says, "I see how hard you worked, and I'm proud of you. Now let's figure out together what comes next."
