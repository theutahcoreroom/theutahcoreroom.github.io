5 try-it-tomorrow engagement tricks for when your students have mentally checked out.

End-of-year engagement ideas that take zero prep and actually work — from a teacher who's been there.

UC
The Utah Core Room
Utah educator & resource creator

It's the last stretch of the school year. The weather is beautiful, the playground is calling, and your carefully planned lesson on text structure is competing with a bee that got into the classroom. The bee is winning.

If your students have mentally packed their backpacks for summer while you're still trying to teach content, welcome to the club. Here are 5 engagement strategies you can use literally tomorrow. No prep. No special materials. No TpT purchase required. (Although I do have some great resources if you want them. Just saying.)

1. The 2-minute partner teach

After teaching a concept, say: "Turn to your partner. You have 2 minutes to teach them what I just taught you. Go." Then watch the room transform. Students who were half-asleep suddenly sit up because they have to perform. The partner who's listening suddenly pays attention because a peer is talking to them, not the teacher they've been tuning out since February.

Why it works: Teaching something requires a deeper level of processing than just hearing it. It also immediately reveals who understood and who's bluffing. You can circulate and listen for misconceptions in real-time instead of discovering them on the assessment.

Pro tip: Give the "teacher" partner a specific sentence starter: "The most important thing about [topic] is..." This prevents the classic "I dunno, what do you think?" deflection.

2. Stand up, sit down voting

Replace hand-raising with full-body movement. Ask a question with two possible answers: "Stand up if you think the main idea is A. Stay seated if you think it's B." The physical movement wakes up the body, the public commitment creates accountability, and you get an instant visual assessment of the whole class.

I use this during RISE review all the time. "Stand up if the answer to this Part A question is C. Stay seated if you chose D." Then I ask one student standing and one sitting to defend their answer using text evidence. Suddenly a multiple-choice review feels like a courtroom drama, and everyone is invested.

Variation: "Move to the left wall if you agree, right wall if you disagree, stand in the middle if you're undecided." The undecided students are gold — they're the ones actively thinking it through.

3. The countdown challenge

"You have 60 seconds to write down everything you remember about [topic]. Go." Set a visible timer. Watch the urgency kick in. Students who won't write a paragraph in 20 minutes will furiously scribble for 60 seconds because the constraint changes the psychology — it's a game, not an assignment.

After the timer, have students compare lists with a partner and add anything they missed in a different color. Now they've reviewed content, identified gaps, and collaborated — all in under 3 minutes.

I sometimes extend this into a class competition: "Which table group can collectively list the most vocabulary terms from our RISE review in 90 seconds?" The group energy is incredible. Kids who "don't care" suddenly care deeply about beating Table 3.

4. Mystery question envelope

Write review questions on slips of paper and put them in an envelope taped to the board. At random moments throughout the day, dramatically pause your lesson and say: "It's time for... THE ENVELOPE." Pull out a question. Whoever answers correctly gets a small privilege — first in line for lunch, chooses the brain break, picks the read-aloud.

The randomness is the magic. Students stay alert because the envelope could strike at any time. I've had students remind ME about the envelope on days I forgot. When kids are begging you to quiz them, you've won.

Total prep time: 5 minutes to write questions. If you're using my RISE review resources, just pull questions directly from the task cards. Done.

5. The "I used to think... now I think..." exit ticket

At the end of a lesson, give students this sentence frame: "I used to think ______. Now I think ______." It takes 2 minutes and produces the most insightful student responses you'll see all year. It works for any subject: "I used to think the main idea was the first sentence. Now I think it's the idea that ALL the details support."

What makes this different from a regular exit ticket: it asks students to notice their own growth. In April, when everyone is exhausted and feeling like nothing matters, this little exercise reminds students (and you) that learning IS happening, even when it doesn't feel like it.

Bonus: save these and read them on your worst day. A stack of "I used to think... now I think..." responses is the best antidote to end-of-year cynicism I've ever found.

The real secret

None of these strategies require special technology, a Pinterest-perfect classroom, or 3 hours of weekend prep. They require one thing: your willingness to try something slightly different when the usual approach stops working. That's not a failure — that's good teaching.

The best teachers I know aren't the ones with the fanciest resources. They're the ones who notice when engagement drops and pivot. You noticed. You're here. That already puts you ahead.
