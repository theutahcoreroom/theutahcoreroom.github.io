Teacher Wellness April 2026 6 min read

You're not lazy — you're depleted. A real guide to surviving end-of-year burnout.

Easy exercises, brain-friendly food, and mindful practices that actually fit into a teacher's life.

UC
The Utah Core Room
Utah educator & resource creator

Let's skip the part where I tell you to "practice self-care" like that means anything when you have 847 things on your to-do list and your classroom smells like a combination of dry-erase markers and existential dread.

It's April. Or maybe May. Honestly, every day after spring break blurs together into one long, fluorescent-lit marathon of testing prep, field trip permission slips, and students who have collectively decided that rules are more like gentle suggestions. If you're running on caffeine, adrenaline, and the faint hope that summer exists — this post is for you.

I've been teaching in Utah long enough to know that end-of-year burnout isn't a personal failure. It's a structural inevitability. You've been sprinting since August. Your body knows it even if your lesson plans don't.

Move your body (but make it embarrassingly easy)

I'm not going to tell you to start a gym routine in April. That's a January lie we tell ourselves. Instead, here are movements that actually work for people who stand on their feet all day and collapse onto the couch at 4:30 PM:

The parking lot loop: Park at the far end of the lot and walk the perimeter once before you go inside. It takes 4 minutes. You'll get sunlight on your face before the fluorescent lights claim you for the day. On days when I do this, I genuinely start the morning calmer. On days I don't, I'm snapping at the copier by 7:45.

Desk stretches between classes: Stand up. Reach for the ceiling like a student who actually knows the answer. Hold it for 10 seconds. Roll your shoulders backward 5 times. That's it. Your neck has been craned over papers for 6 hours — it deserves these 30 seconds.

The after-school walk: Not a jog. Not a hike. A walk. Fifteen minutes around your neighborhood with no podcast, no audiobook, no phone calls. Just your feet and whatever birds are brave enough to live near a school zone. This one genuinely changed my evenings.

Eat like you're fueling a human, not a vending machine

Teacher eating habits in April are a special kind of chaos. You had half a granola bar at 10:15, three of a student's birthday cupcakes at noon, and called that lunch. By 3 PM you're shaky and irritable and wondering why.

Here's what actually helped me when I started paying attention: overnight oats prepped Sunday night (5 minutes of work, breakfast handled for the week), a bag of almonds in your desk drawer that you eat BEFORE you're starving, and an actual lunch that includes protein. I know a turkey wrap isn't exciting. But eating real food at noon is the difference between "I can handle this" and "I'm going to cry in the supply closet."

Also, and I say this with love: you need water. Not coffee. Water. I bought a 32-ounce water bottle with time markers on the side and it was the most annoyingly effective purchase I've ever made. My headaches dropped by half in a week.

Mindful awareness (the teacher-friendly version)

I used to roll my eyes at mindfulness. Then I tried something so small it felt almost pointless — and it turned out to be the most helpful thing on this list.

The transition breath: Before you walk into your classroom each period, stop at the door. Take one breath in through your nose (4 seconds), hold it (4 seconds), release through your mouth (6 seconds). One breath. That's the whole practice. What it does is create a tiny gap between "what just happened" and "what's about to happen." It stops the emotional pile-up that makes the afternoon feel unbearable.

The 3-thing scan: At the end of each day, before you leave your classroom, name 3 things that went right. Not big things. "Marcus raised his hand instead of shouting." "The projector worked on the first try." "Nobody cried, including me." Write them on a sticky note if you want. This practice doesn't fix burnout — but it slows the spiral of feeling like nothing is working.

The honest check-in: Once a week, ask yourself: "Am I tired, or am I depleted?" Tired is normal in April. Depleted means you need to actually cancel something, say no to something, or ask for help with something. Teachers are trained to power through. Sometimes the bravest thing is admitting you can't keep doing it all.

The truth nobody says out loud

You are not behind. You are not failing. You are doing one of the hardest jobs that exists, during the hardest stretch of the year, and the fact that you're still showing up — still reading articles about how to do it better — tells me everything I need to know about the kind of teacher you are.

Summer is coming. You will make it. And next year, maybe we start some of these habits in August instead of scrambling for them in April. But for now? Walk to the parking lot. Drink the water. Breathe at the door. You've got this.
