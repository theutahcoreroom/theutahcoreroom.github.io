Test Prep March 2026 7 min read

Testing season is hard on everyone. Here are 10 strategies — 5 for teachers, 5 for parents.

Practical, empathetic approaches to state test prep that actually reduce stress instead of adding to it.

UC
The Utah Core Room
Utah educator & resource creator

There's a moment every spring when the energy in an elementary school shifts. You can feel it in the hallways, in the teacher's lounge, at pickup. It's the moment everyone realizes: the test is coming.

Teachers feel the weight of data, evaluations, and the knowledge that a single test will attempt to measure an entire year of work. Parents feel the anxiety of not knowing how to help — or worse, the fear that they've somehow not done enough. And caught in the middle are the kids, who absorb all of that adult stress like emotional sponges.

I've spent years teaching in Utah and preparing students for the RISE assessment. Here's what I've learned: the best test prep isn't about drilling more questions. It's about creating conditions where kids can actually show what they know. That means managing the emotional climate as carefully as the academic content.

5 strategies for classroom teachers

Strategy 1: Normalize the test without dramatizing it. Talk about the RISE assessment the same way you'd talk about a field trip — it's something that's happening, here's what to expect, and we're going to be fine. The moment you treat it like a high-stakes, anxiety-inducing event, your students will too. I like to say: "This test is one way to show what you've learned this year. It's not the only way, and it doesn't define how smart you are."

Strategy 2: Teach the format, not just the content. Many students who struggle on state tests actually know the material — they get tripped up by unfamiliar question types. Spend dedicated time teaching what Part A/Part B questions look like, how to approach multiple-select questions, and what "select all that apply" really means. When the format feels familiar, the anxiety drops significantly. My RISE practice tests are specifically designed for this — every question mirrors the actual format so nothing on test day feels like a surprise.

Strategy 3: Build review into daily routines, not "test prep week." The worst thing you can do is stop regular instruction for two weeks of intensive review. Students burn out, behavior problems spike, and the message you send is: "This test is so scary we had to stop everything for it." Instead, weave 10-minute review activities into your existing schedule — bell work, exit tickets, quick partner discussions. Slow and steady always beats the last-minute cram.

Strategy 4: Create a "testing toolkit" with your students. A week before the test, build a toolkit together. Include strategies like: read the question before the passage, eliminate obviously wrong answers first, use the process of elimination on "select all" questions, and re-read the specific paragraph when a question asks about a detail. Write these on an anchor chart and practice them daily. On test day, students have internalized the strategies instead of panicking.

Strategy 5: Protect your own emotional state. Your students can read your stress like a book. If you're panicked about test scores, they'll feel it. Before testing week, give yourself permission to accept that you've done your best. Take the pressure off your own shoulders first, and watch how much calmer your classroom becomes.

5 strategies for parents

Strategy 1: Ask about the test format, not just the score. Most parents have never seen a RISE question. Ask your child's teacher: "What types of questions will my child see?" Understanding the format helps you practice more effectively at home. When your child says "I don't get it," they might mean they don't get the question type, not the skill. That's a much easier fix.

Strategy 2: Read together — but differently. During the weeks before testing, shift your reading routine slightly. After reading a page together, ask: "What's the main idea of what we just read?" or "What evidence in the text tells you the character feels that way?" These are literally the skills being tested. You don't need special materials. Any book, any article, any cereal box can become practice.

Strategy 3: Practice doesn't mean worksheets. You can build test-ready skills into everyday life without a single printout. At dinner: "Can you summarize your day in three sentences?" At the store: "Which of these two products is the better deal and why?" In the car: "What do you think will happen next in that book you're reading?" These conversations exercise the same mental muscles the test measures.

Strategy 4: Manage your own anxiety first. Kids absorb parental stress instantly. If you're worried about test scores, your child will worry about test scores. Try replacing "Are you ready for the test?" with "What's something cool you learned this week?" The goal is to communicate confidence in your child's ability, even if you're privately nervous.

Strategy 5: Focus on sleep, food, and normalcy. The week of testing, your most powerful move is keeping life normal. A solid bedtime routine, a real breakfast (protein helps focus more than sugar), and a calm morning routine will do more for test performance than any last-minute review session. The research is clear: well-rested kids with stable blood sugar outperform stressed kids who crammed the night before. Every single time.

The bigger picture

Here's what I want both teachers and parents to remember: this test is one snapshot of one day. It doesn't capture your child's creativity, kindness, resilience, or the way they light up when they finally understand something. It doesn't measure the teacher who stayed late to help a struggling reader, or the parent who read bedtime stories every single night for years.

Prepare for it? Absolutely. Let it define anyone's worth? Never.
